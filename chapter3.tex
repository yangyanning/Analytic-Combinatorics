\noindent
$\rhd$ \textbf{III.7.} \textit{Largest summands in compositions}.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
Let $M$ be the largest summand in a random composition. We want to show that for any $\epsilon \in (0, 1)$, as $n \to \infty$:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{P}\left((1 - \epsilon)\log_{2}{n} \leq M \leq (1 + \epsilon)\log_{2}{n}\right) \to 1
\end{equation*}

\noindent
\textbf{Upper bound: first moment method}

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
Let $r_{+} = \lfloor(1 + \epsilon)\log_{2}{n}\rfloor$. If $M \geq r_{+}$ then there exists at least one summand of size $\geq r_{+}$.
The number of such summands is $S = \sum_{k = r_{+}}^{\infty}X_{k}$ where $X_{k}$ is the number of summands of size exactly $k$. By Markov's inequality:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{P}(M \geq r_{+}) = \mathbb{P}(S \geq 1) \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[S]}{1} = \sum_{k = r_{+}}^{\infty}\mathbb{E}[X_{k}]
\end{equation*}
Proposition III.4 gives $E[X_{k}] = \frac{n - k + 3}{2^{k + 1}} \leq \frac{n}{2^{k}}$. Then (noting that $X_{k} = 0$ for $k > n$)
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{E}[S] \leq \sum_{k = r_{+}}^{n}\frac{n}{2^{k}} < \frac{n}{2^{r_{+} - 1}}
        < \frac{n}{2^{(1 + \epsilon)\log_{2}{n} - 2}} = 4n^{-\epsilon} \to 0
\end{equation*}
Since $M > (1 + \epsilon)\log_{2}{n}$ implies $M \geq \lfloor (1 + \epsilon)\log_{2}{n} \rfloor = r_{+}$, we have
$\mathbb{P}(M > (1 + \epsilon)\log_{2}{n}) \leq \mathbb{P}(M \geq r_{+}) \to 0$.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
\textbf{Lower bound: second moment method}

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
We have a random composition of $n$, given by $n - 1$ independent gaps, each a ``cut'' with probability $1/2$.
Let $r_{-} = \lceil (1 - \epsilon)\log_{2}{n} \rceil$. Cut the $n$ ones into $m = \left\lfloor \frac{n}{r_{-}} \right\rfloor$ disjoint blocks
of length $r_{-}$, ignoring leftover $1$'s. Inside a block of length $r_{-}$ there are $r_{-} - 1$ internal gaps. The block contains \textbf{no cut} internally
with probability $p = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{r_{-} - 1}$, independently for each block (the gaps of different blocks are disjoint and independent).

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
Define the indicator random variables
\begin{equation*}
    I_{i} = \begin{cases}
        1 & \text{if block} \; $i$ \; \text{has no internal cuts} \\
        0 & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}
for $i = 1, \cdots, m$. Let $Y = \sum_{i = 1}^{m}I_{i}$ which is the number of blocks that are completely uncut internally. If $Y \geq 1$ then
at least one block is a single summand of size at least $r_{-}$. Therefore $\mathbb{P}(M \geq r_{-}) \geq \mathbb{P}(Y \geq 1) = \mathbb{P}(Y > 0)$.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
By Paley-Zygmund inequality (we'll give a short proof later):
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{P}(Y > 0) \geq \frac{(\mathbb{E}[Y])^{2}}{\mathbb{E}[Y^{2}]}
\end{equation*}
Now $Y \sim \text{Binomial}(m,p)$. The first moment:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{E}[Y] = mp = \left\lfloor \frac{n}{r_{-}} \right\rfloor \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{r_{-} - 1}
        > \left( \frac{n}{(1 - \epsilon)\log_{2}{n} + 1} - 1 \right) \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^{(1 - \epsilon)\log_{2}{n}}
        \sim \frac{n^{\epsilon}}{(1 - \epsilon)\log_{2}{n}} \to \infty
\end{equation*}

The second moment $\mathbb{E}[Y^{2}] = Var(Y) + (\mathbb{E}[Y])^{2}$, $Var(Y) = mp(1 - p) \leq mp = \mathbb{E}[Y]$.
Plugging into Paley-Zygmund:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{P}(Y > 0) \geq \frac{(\mathbb{E}[Y])^{2}}{\mathbb{E}[Y^{2}]} = \frac{(\mathbb{E}[Y])^{2}}{Var(Y) + (\mathbb{E}[Y])^{2}}
            \geq \frac{(\mathbb{E}[Y])^{2}}{\mathbb{E}[Y] + (\mathbb{E}[Y])^{2}} = \frac{1}{1 + \frac{1}{\mathbb{E}[Y]}} \to 1
\end{equation*}
Since $M \geq r_{-} = \lceil (1 - \epsilon)\log_{2}{n} \rceil$ implies $M \geq (1 - \epsilon)\log_{2}{n}$, we have
$\mathbb{P}(M \geq (1 - \epsilon)\log_{2}{n}) \geq \mathbb{P}(M \geq r_{-}) \geq \mathbb{P}(Y > 0) \to 1$.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
Finally $\mathbb{P}\left((1 - \epsilon)\log_{2}{n} \leq M \leq (1 + \epsilon)\log_{2}{n}\right) =
    \mathbb{P}(M \geq (1 - \epsilon)\log_{2}{n}) - \mathbb{P}(M > (1 + \epsilon)\log_{2}{n}) \to 1 - 0 = 1$.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
\textbf{Direct Proof of}
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{P}(Y > 0) \geq \frac{(\mathbb{E}[Y])^{2}}{\mathbb{E}[Y^{2}]}
\end{equation*}
Start with the expectation of $Y$ and split it according to whether $Y > 0$:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{E}[Y] = \mathbb{E}[Y \cdot \textbf{1}_{Y > 0}] + \mathbb{E}[Y \cdot \textbf{1}_{Y = 0}] = \mathbb{E}[Y \cdot \textbf{1}_{Y > 0}]
\end{equation*}
Apply the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{E}[Y] \leq \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[Y^{2}]} \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[\textbf{1}_{Y > 0}]} = \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[Y^{2}]} \sqrt{\mathbb{P}(Y > 0)}
\end{equation*}
Because all terms are nonnegative, we can square both sides to obtain
\begin{equation*}
    (\mathbb{E}[Y])^{2} \leq \mathbb{E}[Y^{2}] \cdot \mathbb{P}(Y > 0) \implies \mathbb{P}(Y > 0) \geq \frac{(\mathbb{E}[Y])^{2}}{\mathbb{E}[Y^{2}]}
\end{equation*}

$\rhd$ \textbf{III.10.} \textit{A hundred prisoners II}.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
We show the ``randomization'' trick with an example. Imagine there are 10 drawers sitting in a row. The director maliciously arranged them in one giant
cycle $1 \rightarrow 2 \rightarrow 3 \rightarrow \cdots \rightarrow 10 \rightarrow 1$ to ensure that if any prisoner opens only 5 drawers, they will
never find their own number: Prisoner 1 opens Drawer 1, finds ``2'', goes to Drawer 2, finds ``3'', and so on. He won't find ``1'' until the 10th drawer.
Since he can only open 5, he fails.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
Before the game, the prisoners agree on a random remapping. Let's say their secret permutation is: ``Draw 1 is 7, 2 is 3, 3 is 9, 4 is 1, 5 is 8,
6 is 2, 7 is 10, 8 is 4, 9 is 6, 10 is 5''. Now when a prisoner thinks ``I am opening Drawer $n$'', they actually go to the physical drawer assigned to
$n$ in their secret code. Each prisoner now follows the cycle strategy using their secret map:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Prisoner 1
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Starts at ``Drawer 1'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 7.
        \item Finds Card 8.
        \item Goes to ``Drawer 8'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 4.
        \item Finds Card 5.
        \item Goes to ``Drawer 5'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 8.
        \item Finds Card 9.
        \item Goes to ``Drawer 9'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 6.
        \item Finds Card 7.
        \item Goes to ``Drawer 7'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 10.
        \item Finds Card 1. Success! Prisoner 1 found his number in 5 steps.
    \end{itemize}

    \item Prisoner 2
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Starts at ``Drawer 2'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 3.
        \item Finds Card 4.
        \item Goes to ``Drawer 4'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 1.
        \item Finds Card 2. Success! Prisoner 2 found his number in 2 steps.
    \end{itemize}

    \item Prisoner 3
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Starts at ``Drawer 3'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 9.
        \item Finds Card 10.
        \item Goes to ``Drawer 10'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 5.
        \item Finds Card 6.
        \item Goes to ``Drawer 6'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 2.
        \item Finds Card 3. Success! Prisoner 3 found his number in 3 steps.
    \end{itemize}

    \item Prisoner 4
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Starts at ``Drawer 4'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 1.
        \item Finds Card 2.
        \item Goes to ``Drawer 2'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 3.
        \item Finds Card 4. Success! Prisoner 4 found his number in 2 steps.
    \end{itemize}

    \item Prisoner 5
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Starts at ``Drawer 5'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 8.
        \item Finds Card 9.
        \item Goes to ``Drawer 9'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 6.
        \item Finds Card 7.
        \item Goes to ``Drawer 7'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 10.
        \item Finds Card 1.
        \item Goes to ``Drawer 1'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 7.
        \item Finds Card 8.
        \item Goes to ``Drawer 8'' $\rightarrow$ goes to Physical Drawer 4.
        \item Finds Card 5. Success! Prisoner 5 found his number in 5 steps.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{\textit{Example} III.14.} \textit{Leaves and node types in binary trees}.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
Actually we are dealing with the class $\bar{\mathcal{B}}$ of $\textit{pruned binary trees}$ (see \textbf{\textit{Example} I.13.}):
\begin{equation*}
    \hat{B}(z) = C(z) - 1 = \frac{1 - \sqrt{1 - 4z}}{2z} - 1 = \frac{1 - 2z - \sqrt{1 - 4z}}{2z}
\end{equation*}
where $C(z)$ is the OGF for the Catalan numbers: $C(z) = 1 + zC(z)^{2}$.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
\textbf{Leaves}
\begin{equation*}
    \hat{B}(z, u) = zu + 2z\hat{B}(z, u) + z\hat{B}(z, u)^{2} \implies
        \frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u} = z + 2z\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u} + 2z\hat{B}\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u} \implies
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u} = \frac{z}{1 - 2z - 2z\hat{B}} \implies
        \left.\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u}\right|_{u = 1} = \frac{z}{1 - 2z - 2z\hat{B}(z)} = \frac{z}{\sqrt{1 - 4z}} \implies
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    [z^{n}]\left.\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u}\right|_{u = 1} = [z^{n - 1}](1 - 4z)^{-1/2} = \binom{2n - 2}{n - 1} = \frac{(2n - 2)!}{(n - 1)!^{2}}
\end{equation*}

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
\textbf{1-nodes}
\begin{equation*}
    \hat{B}(z, u) = z + 2zu\hat{B}(z, u) + z\hat{B}(z, u)^{2} \implies
        \frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u} = 2z\hat{B} + 2zu\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u} + 2z\hat{B}\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u} \implies
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u} = \frac{2z\hat{B}}{1 - 2zu - 2z\hat{B}} \implies
        \left.\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u}\right|_{u = 1} = \frac{2z\hat{B}(z)}{1 - 2z - 2z\hat{B}(z)} = \frac{1 - 2z}{\sqrt{1 - 4z}} - 1 \implies
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    [z^{n}]\left.\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u}\right|_{u = 1} = [z^{n}]\left((1 - 4z)^{-1/2} - 2\frac{z}{\sqrt{1 - 4z}}\right)
        = \binom{2n}{n} - 2\binom{2n - 2}{n - 1}
\end{equation*}

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
\textbf{2-nodes}
\begin{equation*}
    \hat{B}(z, u) = z + 2z\hat{B}(z, u) + zu\hat{B}(z, u)^{2} \implies
        \frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u} = 2z\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u} + z\hat{B}^{2} + 2zu\hat{B}\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u} \implies
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u} = \frac{z\hat{B}^{2}}{1 - 2z - 2zu\hat{B}} \implies
        \left.\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u}\right|_{u = 1} = \frac{z\hat{B}(z)^{2}}{1 - 2z - 2z\hat{B}(z)}
        = \frac{z\hat{B}(z)^{2}}{\sqrt{1 - 4z}} = \frac{z^{3}C(z)^{4}}{\sqrt{1 - 4z}}
\end{equation*}
Since $C'(z)$ satisfies $C'(z) = C(z)^{2} + 2zC(z)C'(z)$, we have
\begin{equation*}
    C'(z) = \frac{C(z)^{2}}{1 - 2zC(z)} = \frac{C(z)^{2}}{\sqrt{1 - 4z}}
\end{equation*}
Plugging this into our equation:
\begin{equation*}
    \left.\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u}\right|_{u = 1} = z^{3}C(z)^{2} \cdot \frac{C(z)^{2}}{\sqrt{1 - 4z}}
        = z^{3}C(z)^{2}C'(z) = \frac{z^{3}}{3}\frac{d}{dz}C(z)^{3} = z^{3}\frac{d}{dz}F(z)
\end{equation*}
where $F(z) = \frac{1}{3}C(z)^{3}$.
Now
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        [z^{n}]\left.\frac{\partial \hat{B}}{\partial u}\right|_{u = 1}
        &= [z^{n}]z^{3}\frac{d}{dz}F(z) \\
        &= [z^{n - 3}]\frac{d}{dz}F(z) \\
        &= (n - 2)F_{n - 2} \\
        &= \frac{n - 2}{3}[z^{n - 2}]C(z)^{3} \\
        &= \frac{n - 2}{3} \cdot \frac{3}{n + 1}\binom{2n - 2}{n - 2} \\
        &= \binom{2n - 2}{n - 3}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
Consequently, number of \textbf{Leaves} + number of \textbf{1-nodes} + number of \textbf{2-nodes} is
\begin{equation*}
    \binom{2n - 2}{n - 1} + \binom{2n}{n} - 2\binom{2n - 2}{n - 1} + \binom{2n - 2}{n - 3} = \frac{n}{n + 1}\binom{2n}{n}
\end{equation*}
as expected.

$\rhd$ \textbf{III.18.} \textit{Node-degree profile in simple varieties of trees}.
\begin{equation*}
    T(z, u) = z\left(\phi(T(z, u)) + \phi_{k}(u - 1)T(z, u)^{k}\right)
\end{equation*}
Differentiating with respect to $u$ gives
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial T}{\partial u} =
        z\left(\phi'(T)\frac{\partial T}{\partial u} + \phi_{k}T^{k} + k\phi_{k}(u - 1)T^{k - 1}\frac{\partial T}{\partial u}\right) \implies
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    \Omega(z) = z\left(\phi'(T(z))\Omega(z) + \phi_{k}T(z)^{k}\right) = z\frac{\phi_{k}T(z)^{k}}{1 - z\phi'(T(z))}
\end{equation*}
Since $T(z) = z\phi(T(z))$, differentiating with respect to $z$ yields
\begin{equation*}
    T'(z) = \phi(T(z)) + z\phi'(T(z))T'(z) \implies 1 = \frac{\phi(T(z))}{T'(z)} + z\phi'(T(z))
\end{equation*}
Hence
\begin{equation*}
    \Omega(z) = \phi_{k}zT(z)^{k} \cdot \frac{T'(z)}{\phi(T(z))} = \phi_{k}zT(z)^{k} \cdot \frac{T'(z)}{T(z)/z} = \phi_{k}z^{2}T(z)^{k - 1}T'(z)
\end{equation*}
For Cayley trees:
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        n![z^{n}]\Omega(z)
        &= n![z^{n}]\frac{1}{k!}z^{2}\frac{d}{dz}\frac{T(z)^{k}}{k} \\
        &= \frac{n!}{k \cdot k!}[z^{n - 2}]\frac{d}{dz}T(z)^{k} \\
        &= \frac{n!}{k \cdot k!}(n - 1)[z^{n - 1}]T(z)^{k} \\
        &= \frac{n(n - 1)}{k} \cdot (n - 1)![z^{n - 1}]\frac{T(z)^{k}}{k!} \\
        &= \frac{n(n - 1)}{k}\binom{n - 2}{k - 1}(n - 1)^{n - 1 - k} \\
        &= (n - k)\binom{n}{k}(n - 1)^{n - 1 - k}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}

\textbf{\textit{Example} III.15.} \textit{Path length in trees}.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
We want to show
\begin{equation*}
    z^{2}\frac{G'(z)}{(1 - G(z))^{2} - z} = \frac{z}{2(1 - 4z)} - \frac{z}{2\sqrt{1 - 4z}}
\end{equation*}
Express $z$ and $G'$ in terms of $G$:
\begin{equation*}
    G = \frac{z}{1 - G} \implies z = G(1 - G) = G - G^{2}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    z = G - G^{2} \implies 1 = G' - 2GG' \implies G' = \frac{1}{1 - 2G}
\end{equation*}
Hence
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        z^{2}\frac{G'(z)}{(1 - G(z))^{2} - z}
        &= (G(1 - G))^{2}\frac{1/(1 - 2G)}{1 - 2G + G^{2} - (G - G^{2})} \\
        &= \frac{G^{2}(1 - G)^{2}}{(1 - 2G)(1 - 3G + 2G^{2})} \\
        &= \frac{G^{2}(1 - G)}{(1 - 2G)^{2}} \\
        &= \frac{(G - G^{2}) \cdot G}{1 - 4(G - G^{2})} \\
        &= \frac{zG}{1 - 4z} \\
        &= \frac{z}{1 - 4z} \cdot \frac{1 - \sqrt{1 - 4z}}{2} \\
        &= \frac{z}{2}\left(\frac{1}{1 - 4z} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 4z}}\right)
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}

\noindent
\textbf{\textit{Example} III.19.} \textit{Rises in Bernoulli trials: Simon Newcomb's problem}.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
Recurrence for $W$
\begin{equation} \label{eq:III.19.1}
    W_{j} = z_{j} + z_{j}\sum_{k = 1}^{j - 1}W_{k} + vz_{j}\sum_{k = j}^{r}W_{k}
\end{equation}
implies recurrence for $X$
\begin{equation} \label{eq:III.19.2}
    X_{j} = 1 + \sum_{k = 1}^{j - 1}W_{k} + v\sum_{k = j}^{r}W_{k} = 1 + \sum_{k = 1}^{j - 1}z_{k}X_{k} + v\sum_{k = j}^{r}z_{k}X_{k}
\end{equation}
Consequently
\begin{equation*}
    X_{j + 1} - X_{j} = W_{j} - vW_{j} = z_{j}X_{j}(1 - v) \implies X_{j + 1} = X_{j}(1 + z_{j}(1 - v))
\end{equation*}
By induction (unfolding the recurrence), we can express any $X_{j}$ in terms of $X_{1}$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:III.19.3}
    X_{j} = X_{1}\prod_{k = 1}^{j - 1}(1 + (1 - v)z_{k})
\end{equation}
To find $X_{1}$, we look back at \ref{eq:III.19.1} specifically for the case $j = 1$:
\begin{equation*}
    W_{1} = z_{1} + z_{1} \cdot 0 + vz_{1}\sum_{k = 1}^{r}W_{k} \implies z_{1}X_{1} = z_{1} + vz_{1}\sum_{k = 1}^{r}z_{k}X_{k}
    \implies X_{1} = 1 + v\sum_{k = 1}^{r}z_{k}X_{k}
\end{equation*}
If we set $j = r + 1$ in \ref{eq:III.19.2} (introduce $X_{r + 1}$ such that \ref{eq:III.19.2} holds), the second sum vanishes, giving us
\begin{equation*}
    X_{r + 1} = 1 + \sum_{k = 1}^{r}z_{k}X_{k} \implies \sum_{k = 1}^{r}z_{k}X_{k} = X_{r + 1} - 1 \implies X_{1} = 1 + v(X_{r + 1} - 1)
\end{equation*}
The total generating function is
\begin{equation*}
    W = 1 + \sum_{k = 1}^{r}W_{k} = 1 + \sum_{k = 1}^{r}z_{k}X_{k} = X_{r + 1}
\end{equation*}
We also know from \ref{eq:III.19.3} that
\begin{equation*}
    X_{r + 1} = X_{1}\prod_{j = 1}^{r}(1 + (1 - v)z_{j}) = X_{1}P
\end{equation*}
Finally
\begin{equation*}
    X_{1} = 1 + v(X_{r + 1} - 1) = 1 + v(X_{1}P - 1) \implies X_{1} = \frac{1 - v}{1 - vP} \implies
        W = \frac{P(1 - v)}{1 - vP}
\end{equation*}

\vspace{\baselineskip}

\begin{equation*}

To derive the functional equation, note that
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        f(z) &= z + z^{2} + \cdots = \frac{z}{1 - z} \\
        \mathcal{L}[F(z, u)]
            &= \mathcal{L}[\sum_{j}f^{\langle j \rangle}(z)u^{j}] \\
            &= \sum_{j}f^{\langle j \rangle}(z)\mathcal{L}[u^{j}] \\
            &= \sum_{j}f^{\langle j \rangle}(z)\frac{u^{j}}{1 - u} \\
            &= \frac{1}{1 - u}F(z, u)
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
Hence
\begin{equation*}
    F(z, u) = f(zu) + (\mathcal{L}[F(z, u)])_{u \mapsto zu} = \frac{zu}{1 - zu} + \frac{1}{1 - zu}F(z, zu)
\end{equation*}

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
Now we demonstrate the equivalence of the two alternative forms of $F(z, u)$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:III.22.1}
    F(z, u) = \frac{zu}{1 - zu} + \frac{z^{2}u}{(1 - zu)(1 - z^{2}u)} + \frac{z^{3}u}{(1 - zu)(1 - z^{2}u)(1 - z^{3}u)} + \cdots
\end{equation}
\begin{equation} \label{eq:III.22.2}
    F(z, u) = \frac{zu}{1 - z} + \frac{z^{2}u^{2}}{(1 - z)(1 - z^{2})} + \frac{z^{3}u^{3}}{(1 - z)(1 - z^{2})(1 - z^{3})} + \cdots
\end{equation}
\ref{eq:III.22.1} is obtained by repeated substitution. While \ref{eq:III.22.2} can be verified via Taylor expansion, it is a bit mechanical.
Let's try a purely algebraic approach. Assume $F(z, u) = \sum_{k \geq 1}A_{k}(z)u^{k}$ and plug it into the rearranged functional equation:
\begin{equation*}
    (1 - zu)F(z, u) = zu + F(z, zu)
\end{equation*}
Expand both sides:
\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{k \geq 1}A_{k}(z)u^{k} - zu\left(\sum_{k \geq 1}A_{k}(z)u^{k}\right) = zu + \sum_{k \geq 1}A_{k}(z)(zu)^{k}
\end{equation*}
Align the powers of $u$:
\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{k \geq 1}A_{k}(z)u^{k} - \sum_{k \geq 1}A_{k}(z)zu^{k + 1} = zu + \sum_{k \geq 1}A_{k}(z)z^{k}u^{k}
\end{equation*}
Extract the coefficients of $u^{k}$ (for $k > 1$):
\begin{equation*}
    A_{k}(z) - zA_{k - 1}(z) = z^{k}A_{k}(z) \implies A_{k}(z) = \frac{z}{1 - z^{k}}A_{k - 1}(z)
\end{equation*}
Starting from the base case $A_{1}(z) = z + z^{2} + \cdots = \frac{z}{1 - z}$ (\ref{eq:III.22.1}), the telescoping product gives
\begin{equation*}
    A_{k}(z) = \frac{z}{1 - z^{k}}\frac{z}{1 - z^{k - 1}} \cdots \frac{z}{1 - z^{2}}\frac{z}{1 - z} 
        = \frac{z^{k}}{(1 - z)(1 - z^{2}) \cdots (1 -z^{k})}
\end{equation*}
This confirms the $k$-th term of \ref{eq:III.22.2} is exactly $A_{k}(z)u^{k}$.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
There is a combinatorial interpretation: the $k$-th term of \ref{eq:III.22.1} represents a partition with \textbf{exactly $k$ summands}; while
the $k$-th term of \ref{eq:III.22.2} represents a partition where the \textbf{largest summand is exactly $k$}.
We illustrate the idea with the case $k = 3$.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
The 3rd term in \ref{eq:III.22.1}:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{z^{3}u}{(1 - zu)(1 - z^{2}u)(1 - z^{3}u)} = (zzzu)\sum_{j}(zu)^{j} \sum_{k}z^{k}(zu)^{k} \sum_{l}z^{l}z^{l}(zu)^{l}
\end{equation*}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $z^{1} \cdot z^{1} \cdot (zu)^{1}$ places $1$ cell on all three columns
    \item $z^{0} \cdot z^{0} \cdot (zu)^{j}$ places $j \geq 0$ cells on the rightmost column
    \item $z^{0} \cdot z^{k} \cdot (zu)^{k}$ places $k \geq 0$ cells on the rightmost two columns
    \item $z^{l} \cdot z^{l} \cdot (zu)^{l}$ places $l \geq 0$ cells on all three columns
\end{itemize}
This ``lifting'' mechanism ensures the summands remain weakly increasing.

\vspace{\baselineskip}

The 3rd term in \ref{eq:III.22.2}:
\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $z^{3}u^{3}$ ensures there is at least one part of size $3$ (which also marks the last column height $3$).
    \item $\frac{1}{(1 - z)(1 - z^{2})(1 - z^{3})}$ is the standard generating function for partitions using parts of size $\{1, 2, 3\}$.

\begin{equation*}
    K(z, u) = k(zu) + (\mathcal{L}[K(z, u)])_{u \mapsto zu} = \frac{zu}{1 - zu} + \frac{zu}{1 - zu}K(z, 1) - K(z, zu)
\end{equation*}
To solve for $K(z, 1)$, let $K(z, 1) = X$:
\begin{equation*}
    K(z, u) = \frac{zu}{1 - zu}(1 + X) - K(z, zu)
\end{equation*}
Iterate once:
\begin{equation*}
    K(z, u) = \frac{zu}{1 - zu}(1 + X) - \left(\frac{z^{2}u}{1 - z^{2}u}(1 + X) - K(z, z^{2}u)\right)
\end{equation*}
Iterate infinitely:
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        K(z, u)
        &= \frac{zu}{1 - zu}(1 + X) - \frac{z^{2}u}{1 - z^{2}u}(1 + X) + \frac{z^{3}u}{1 - z^{3}u}(1 + X) - \cdots \\
        &= (1 + X)\sum_{j \geq 1}(-1)^{j - 1}\frac{z^{j}u}{1 - z^{j}u}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
Now set $u = 1$ so that $K(z, 1) = X$:
\begin{equation*}
    X = (1 + X)\sum_{j \geq 1}\frac{(-1)^{j - 1}z^{j}}{1 - z^{j}}
\end{equation*}
Let the sum be $S$:
\begin{equation*}
    X = (1 + X)S \implies 1 + X = \frac{1}{1 - S} = \left(1 - \sum_{j \geq 1}\frac{(-1)^{j - 1}z^{j}}{1 - z^{j}}\right)^{-1}
        = \left(1 + \sum_{j \geq 1}\frac{(-z)^{j}}{1 - z^{j}}\right)^{-1}
\end{equation*}

For simplicity, let's denote $\hat{I}(z, \textbf{u})$ by $I$. The differential equation becomes

Separating variables yields

\end{equation*}
which we integrate from the initial condition $I(0) = 0$ to a general $z$:
\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}
Inserting $I(0) = 0$ and writing $I(z)$ simply as $I$ for brevity:
\begin{equation*}
    \int_{0}^{I}\frac{d\xi}{u_{0} + 2v_{1}\xi + u_{2}\xi^{2}} = \int_{0}^{z}d\zeta = z
\end{equation*}
Completing the square in the denominator:
\begin{equation*}
    u_{0} + 2v_{1}\xi + u_{2}\xi^{2}
    = u_{2}\left[\left(\xi + \frac{v_{1}}{u_{2}}\right)^{2} + \frac{\delta^{2}}{u_{2}^{2}}\right]
\end{equation*}
and substituting this into the integral gives
\begin{equation*}
    \int_{0}^{I}\frac{d\xi}{u_{2}\left[\left(\xi + \frac{v_{1}}{u_{2}}\right)^{2} + \left(\frac{\delta}{u_{2}}\right)^{2}\right]} = z
\end{equation*}
Using the standard integral $\int\frac{dx}{x^{2} +a^{2}} = \frac{1}{a}\text{arctan}\frac{x}{a}$:
\begin{equation*}
    z = \frac{1}{u_{2}}\frac{1}{\frac{\delta}{u_{2}}}
        \text{arctan}\left.\frac{\xi + \frac{v_{1}}{u_{2}}}{\frac{\delta}{u_{2}}}\right|_{\xi = 0}^{\xi = I}

            \text{arctan}\left(\frac{u_{2}I + v_{1}}{\delta}\right) - \text{arctan}\left(\frac{v_{1}}{\delta}\right)
        \right]
\end{equation*}
Rearranging and taking the tangent of both sides, we obtain
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{u_{2}I + v_{1}}{\delta} = \text{tan}\left[\text{arctan}\left(\frac{v_{1}}{\delta}\right) + z\delta\right]
        = \frac{\frac{v_{1}}{\delta} + \text{tan}(z\delta)}{1 - \frac{v_{1}}{\delta}\text{tan}(z\delta)}

\end{equation*}
Solving for $I$ yields the final expression:
\begin{equation*}

\noindent
\textbf{Unary type}. We consider both unary types simultaneously. Note that $\hat{I}$ depends on $u_{0}, u_{1}$ only through
$v_{1} = (u_{0} + u_{1})/2$. Set $u_{0} = u_{2} = 1$. Then $\delta = \sqrt{1 - v_{1}^{2}}$ and
\begin{equation*}
    \hat{I}(z, v_{1}) = \delta\frac{v_{1} + \delta\text{tan}(z\delta)}{\delta - v_{1}\text{tan}(z\delta)} - v_{1}
        = \frac{v_{1} + \delta^{2}w}{1 - v_{1}w} - v_{1} = \frac{w}{1 - v_{1}w}
\end{equation*}
where $w \coloneqq \frac{\text{tan}(z\delta)}{\delta} = z + \frac{z^{3}}{3}\delta^{2} + O(\delta^{4})$.
We have
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial I}{\partial u_{1}} = \frac{\partial I}{\partial u_{1}'} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial I}{\partial v_{1}}
\end{equation*}
We compute $\frac{\partial I}{\partial v_{1}}$ at $v_{1} = (1 + 1)/2 = 1$. First, $w$ depends on $v_{1}$ via $s \coloneqq \delta^{2} = 1 - v_{1}^{2}$:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial w}{\partial v_{1}} = \frac{\partial w}{\partial s} \cdot (-2v_{1})
\end{equation*}
From the series $\left.\frac{\partial w}{\partial s}\right|_{s = 0} = \frac{z^{3}}{3}$, so at $v_{1} = 1$:
\begin{equation*}
    \left.\frac{\partial w}{\partial v_{1}}\right|_{v_{1} = 1} = -\frac{2z^{3}}{3}
\end{equation*}
Now differentiate $\hat{I}(z, v_{1})$:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial \hat{I}}{\partial v_{1}}
        = \frac{\frac{\partial w}{\partial v_{1}}(1 - v_{1}w) + w(w + v_{1}\frac{\partial w}{\partial v_{1}})}{(1 - v_{1}w)^{2}}
\end{equation*}
Substituting $v_{1} = 1, w = z$ and $\frac{\partial w}{\partial v_{1}} = -\frac{2z^{3}}{3}$, we obtain
\begin{equation*}
    \left.\frac{\partial \hat{I}}{\partial v_{1}}\right|_{v_{1} = 1} = \frac{z^{2}\left(1 - \frac{2}{3}z\right)}{(1 - z)^{2}}
\end{equation*}
Consequently
\begin{equation*}
    \left.\frac{\partial \hat{I}}{\partial u_{1}}\right|_{u_{1} = 1} = \left.\frac{\partial \hat{I}}{\partial u_{1}'}\right|_{u_{1}' = 1}
        = \frac{z^{2}\left(1 - \frac{2}{3}z\right)}{2(1 - z)^{2}}
\end{equation*}
The coefficient of $z^{n}$ in $\left.\frac{\partial \hat{I}}{\partial u_{1}}\right|_{u_{1} = 1}$ and
$\left.\frac{\partial \hat{I}}{\partial u_{1}'}\right|_{u_{1}' = 1}$ is $(n + 1)/6$.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
\textbf{Binary type}. Set $u_{0} = 1$, $v_{1} = (1 + 1)/2 = 1$. Then $\delta = \sqrt{u_{2} - 1}$ and
\begin{equation*}
    \hat{I}(z, u_{2}) = \frac{\delta}{u_{2}}\frac{1 + \delta\text{tan}(z\delta)}{\delta - \text{tan}(z\delta)} - \frac{1}{u_{2}} = \frac{w}{1 - w}
\end{equation*}
where $w \coloneqq \frac{\text{tan}(z\delta)}{\delta} = z + \frac{z^{3}}{3}\delta^{2} + O(\delta^{4})$.
This is similar to the nullary case. From \ref{eq:III.23.1} we have
\begin{equation*}
    \left.\frac{\partial \hat{I}}{\partial u_{2}}\right|_{u_{2} = 1}
    = [\delta^{2}]\frac{w}{1 - w}
    = \frac{z^{3}}{3(1 - z)^{2}}
\end{equation*}
The coefficient of $z^{n}$ in $\left.\frac{\partial \hat{I}}{\partial u_{2}}\right|_{u_{2} = 1}$ is $\frac{n - 2}{3}$.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
Summing the contributions of all node types:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{n + 1}{3} + \frac{n + 1}{6} + \frac{n + 1}{6} + \frac{n - 2}{3} = n
\end{equation*}
as expected.

\noindent
\textbf{\textit{Example} III.25.} \textit{Rises and ascending runs in permutations}.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
\textbf{\textit{Rises}}

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
First, we expand $A(z, u)$ as a power series in $z$ at $z = 0$.
\begin{equation*}
    A(z, u) = \frac{u - 1}{u - e^{z(u - 1)}}
\end{equation*}
The denominator
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        u - e^{z(u - 1)}
        &= u - \left[1 + (u - 1)z + \frac{(u - 1)^{2}}{2}z^{2} + \frac{(u - 1)^{3}}{6}z^{3} + \frac{(u - 1)^{4}}{24}z^{4} + O(z^{5})\right] \\
        &= (u - 1) - (u - 1)z - \frac{(u - 1)^{2}}{2}z^{2} - \frac{(u - 1)^{3}}{6}z^{3} - \frac{(u - 1)^{4}}{24}z^{4} + O(z^{5}) \\
        &= (u - 1)\left[1 - z - \frac{u - 1}{2}z^{2} - \frac{(u - 1)^{2}}{6}z^{3} - \frac{(u - 1)^{3}}{24}z^{4} + O(z^{5})\right]
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
Hence
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        A(z, u)
        &= \left(1 - z - \frac{u - 1}{2}z^{2} - \frac{(u - 1)^{2}}{6}z^{3} - \frac{(u - 1)^{3}}{24}z^{4} + O(z^{5})\right)^{-1} \\
        &= \left(1 - z - \frac{u - 1}{2}z^{2} - \frac{(u - 1)^{2}}{6}z^{3} - \frac{(u - 1)^{3}}{24}z^{4}\right)^{-1} + O(z^{5})
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
Set
\begin{equation*}
    w = z + \frac{u - 1}{2}z^{2} + \frac{(u - 1)^{2}}{6}z^{3} + \frac{(u - 1)^{3}}{24}z^{4}
\end{equation*}
Compute powers of $w$ up to $z^{4}$, dropping $O(z^{5})$ terms:
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        w^{2}
        &= \left(z + \frac{u - 1}{2}z^{2} + \frac{(u - 1)^{2}}{6}z^{3}\right)^{2} \\
        &= z^{2} + \left(\frac{u - 1}{2}z^{2}\right)^{2} + 2z\frac{u - 1}{2}z^{2} + 2z\frac{(u - 1)^{2}}{6}z^{3} \\
        &= z^{2} + (u - 1)z^{3} + \frac{7}{12}(u - 1)^{2}z^{4}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    w^{3} = \left(z + \frac{u - 1}{2}z^{2}\right)^{3} = z^{3} + 3z^{2}\frac{u - 1}{2}z^{2} = z^{3} + \frac{3}{2}(u - 1)z^{4}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    w^{4} = z^{4}
\end{equation*}
Then
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        A(z, u)
        &= 1 + z + \left(\frac{u - 1}{2} + 1\right)z^{2} + \left(\frac{(u - 1)^{2}}{6} + (u - 1) + 1\right)z^{3} \\
        &\quad + \left(\frac{(u - 1)^{3}}{24} + \frac{7}{12}(u - 1)^{2} + \frac{3}{2}(u - 1) + 1\right)z^{4} + O(z^{5}) \\
        &= 1 + z + (u + 1)\frac{z^{2}}{2} + (u^{2} + 4u + 1)\frac{z^{3}}{6} + (u^{3} + 11u^{2} + 11u + 1)\frac{z^{4}}{24} + \cdots
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
Now, we expand $A(z, u)$ as a power series in $u$ at $z = 1$. Set $v = u - 1$.
\begin{equation*}
    A(z, u) = \frac{v}{(v + 1) - e^{zv}}
\end{equation*}
The denominator
\begin{equation*}
    v + 1 - e^{zv} = v + 1 - \left(1 + zv + \frac{z^{2}}{2}v^{2} + \frac{z^{3}}{6}v^{3} + O(v^{4})\right)
        = (1 - z)v - \frac{z^{2}}{2}v^{2} - \frac{z^{3}}{6}v^{3} + O(v^{4})
\end{equation*}
Hence
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        A(z, u)
        &= \left(1 - z - \frac{z^{2}}{2}v - \frac{z^{3}}{6}v^{2} + O(v^{3})\right)^{-1} \\
        &= \frac{1}{1 - z}\left(1 - \frac{z^{2}}{2(1 - z)}v - \frac{z^{3}}{6(1 - z)}v^{2} + O(v^{3})\right)^{-1} \\
        &= \frac{1}{1 - z}\left(1 - \frac{z^{2}}{2(1 - z)}v - \frac{z^{3}}{6(1 - z)}v^{2}\right)^{-1} + O(v^{3}) \\
        &= \frac{1}{1 - z}\left(1 + \frac{z^{2}}{2(1 - z)}v + \frac{z^{3}}{6(1 - z)}v^{2} + \left(\frac{z^{2}}{2(1 - z)}v\right)^{2}\right) + O(v^{3}) \\
        &= \frac{1}{1 - z} + \frac{z^{2}}{2(1 - z)^{2}}v + \frac{2z^{3} + z^{4}}{6(1 - z)^{3}}\frac{v^{2}}{2!} + \cdots
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
Extracting coefficients gives
\begin{equation*}
    [z^{n}]\frac{z^{2}}{2(1 - z)^{2}} = \frac{1}{2}[z^{n - 2}]\sum_{m = 0}^{\infty}(m + 1)z^{m} = \frac{n - 1}{2}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    [z^{n}]\frac{2z^{3} + z^{4}}{6(1 - z)^{3}}
        = \frac{1}{3}[z^{n - 3}]\sum_{m = 0}^{\infty}\binom{m + 2}{2}z^{m} + \frac{1}{6}[z^{n - 4}]\sum_{m = 0}^{\infty}\binom{m + 2}{2}z^{m}
        = \frac{3n^{2} - 11n + 10}{12}
\end{equation*}
The mean of the number of rises in a random permutation of size $n$ is $\frac{n - 1}{2}$, the variance is
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{3n^{2} - 11n + 10}{12} + \frac{n - 1}{2} - \left(\frac{n - 1}{2}\right)^{2} = \frac{n + 1}{12}
\end{equation*}

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
\textbf{\textit{Ascending Runs}}

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
The ordinary generating function $I(z,v)$ is an improper rational function: its numerator degree is $l + 1$ and denominator degree is $l$.
By polynomial long division and standard partial fraction decomposition, $I(z, v)$ can be written exactly as:
\begin{equation*}
    I(z, v) = 1 - z + \sum_{j = 1}^{l}\frac{c_{j}}{1 - \omega_{j}z}
\end{equation*}
which doesn't depend on $v$. Why does the formula have $(1 - z)(v + 1)$?

\vspace{\baselineskip}
\noindent
It is not hard to see that low-order polynomial terms ($1$ and $z$) are not present in the $I(z, v)$ expansion ($l \geq 1$) because $I(0, v) = 0$ and 
$\left.\frac{\partial}{\partial z}I(z, v)\right|_{z = 0} = 0$. Now let's expand the partial fraction using the geometric series
$(1 - \omega_{j}z)^{-1} = 1 + \omega_{j}z + \cdots$:
\begin{equation*}
    I(z, v) = 1 - z + \sum_{j = 1}^{l}c_{j}(1 + \omega_{j}z + O(z^{2}))
        = \left(1 + \sum_{j = 1}^{l}c_{j}\right) + \left(-1 + \sum_{j = 1}^{l}c_{j}\omega_{j}\right)z + O(z^{2})
\end{equation*}
Matching the coefficients of $1$ and $z$, we have
\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{j = 1}^{l}c_{j} = -1, \quad \sum_{j = 1}^{l}c_{j}\omega_{j} = 1
\end{equation*}
The shift from 1 and -1 to $(v + 1)$ and $-(v + 1)$ happens when we transition from $I(z, v)$ to $\hat{I}(z, v)$:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{1}{1 - \omega_{j}z} \mapsto e^{\omega_{j}z}
\end{equation*}
If we expand the exponential terms in the definition of $\hat{I}(z, v)$, we get
\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{j = 1}^{l}c_{j}e^{\omega_{j}z} = \sum_{j = 1}^{l}c_{j}(1 + \omega_{j}z + O(z^{2})) = -1 + z + O(z^{2})
\end{equation*}
Now look at Flajolet's full expression for $\hat{I}(z, v)$:
\begin{equation*}
    \hat{I}(z, v) = (1 - z)(v + 1) + \sum_{j = 1}^{l}c_{j}e^{\omega_{j}z}
\end{equation*}
Let's check low-order terms by plugging in our expression:
\begin{equation*}
    \hat{I}(z, v) = v + 1 - zv - z + (-1 + z + O(z^{2})) = v - vz + O(z^{2})
\end{equation*}
I can't see why $I_{0, 1} = v$ and $I_{1, 1} = -1$ \frownie.
